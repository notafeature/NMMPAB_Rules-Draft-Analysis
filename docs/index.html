New Mexico Medical Psilocybin Program · Advisory Board

Training & Education:
The tabled items

Updated July 17, 2026. Reflects the July 17 Advisory Board meeting.

The Training & Education Committee wrote a recommendation for how medical psilocybin providers are trained and permitted. On June 26, 2026 the Advisory Board voted 3-2 to accept it and set three items aside. The board took those three items up at its July 9 meeting and again at its July 17, 2026 meeting.

Where things stand, July 17, 2026

Three items are still open

Each was set aside on June 26 and taken up on July 9 and July 17. Here is where each landed, and what is still ahead.

Item 1

Must the certifying clinician hold a controlled-substance number?

Kept, and going into the rule, July 17

The department is keeping the requirement and putting it into the rule. Dominic Zurlo, July 17: "the department at least is going to move forward from a regulatory standpoint with that as a requirement." It also added new certifying-clinician language to the draft, setting how a patient is evaluated through one in-person or two telemedicine pathways. The board did not vote to remove it. Not final; goes to a public hearing.

The July 17 board meeting, in brief: the board voted 7-0 to send the practicum and didactic hours to the Training and Education Committee; the department is keeping the certifying-clinician controlled-substance-number requirement and revised the exam language.▸

July 17 was a meeting of the full Medical Psilocybin Advisory Board, in the morning. It took up the three items set aside on June 26.

  • Practicum and didactic hours: deferred by a 7-0 vote. The board sent the numbers, and the 20-hour supervisory question, to the Training and Education Committee to develop updated standards. That committee meets at 1 PM today.
  • Certifying-clinician controlled-substance number: kept by the department. Dominic Zurlo said that after consulting the Secretary of Health and the department’s attorneys, the requirement will remain in the rule the department puts forward. The board did not vote to remove it; Chris Peskuski restated his objection that it is an access bottleneck.
  • Certifying-clinician exam: revised language. The department added three ways to meet the in-person requirement (in person within six months, or telemedicine with a records review or a formal consult), to ease rural and end-of-life access. Verbatim text on the CS-number page.
  • Reciprocity stays at December 31, 2027, unchanged from July 9.

Next: Training and Education Committee at 1 PM today; next full board August 14, 1 PM; a rule hearing is targeted for the end of August.

1

The controlled-substance numberCertifying clinician (renamed from "diagnostic clinician")

Should the certifying clinician be required to hold a New Mexico controlled substance number to be permitted?

What this is: the certifying clinician authorizes a patient's entry to the program. A controlled-substance-number requirement limits which clinicians can serve in that role. Clarified on June 26 as a New Mexico CS number, not a federal DEA number. The July 9 draft renames this role from "diagnostic clinician" to "certifying clinician."

Provide input on this item →

What the July 9 draft does

The June 12 recommendation (p. 1) required a New Mexico Controlled Substance number. The July 9 rules draft keeps that requirement (p. 8) and renames the role to "certifying clinician" (p. 1). On July 9 the board sent the requirement back to the department; on July 17 the department said it will keep the requirement in the rule and revised the certifying-clinician exam language.

The July 9 draft renames the role to "certifying clinician" and keeps the controlled-substance requirement. The certifying-clinician application packet requires, among other items:

  • "Documentation of current NM professional license (e.g. MD, NP);" p. 8
  • "Certificate or verification of results NM Controlled Substance number;" p. 8

Where it stands

July 17 meeting: the department said it will keep the controlled-substance-number requirement in the rule, after taking the earlier concerns to the Secretary of Health and its attorneys, and described it as consistent with the medical cannabis program. The board did not vote to remove it. The department also revised the certifying-clinician exam language, adding three ways to meet the in-person requirement. Chris Peskuski restated his objection that the requirement is an access bottleneck and puts liability on a clinician who is not in the room. Larry Leeman noted that federal rescheduling to Schedule III, which he considers likely by mid-2027, could reopen it. The verbatim language and the open threads are on the CS-number page.

July 9 meeting: sent back to the department. The board did not endorse the requirement and asked the department to reconsider and weigh alternatives (a consultation model, self-certification, or behavioral-health referral by telemedicine), citing malpractice liability for certifying clinicians, rural access, and the shortage of eligible providers. The rename to "certifying clinician" is confirmed. No vote was taken.

2

The practicum and didactic hoursPracticum and didactic requirements

Are 100 hours (facilitators) / 120 hours (practitioners) of practicum and roughly 35 didactic hours the right numbers, and should every practitioner complete 20 supervisory hours?

What this is: the practicum is the supervised-practice portion of provider training. The didactic hours are the classroom portion: a 25-hour core module plus a 5-hour role-specific module for facilitators and practitioners (about 35 hours combined, plus 5 more hours of simulated patient experience counted separately) and 8 hours for the certifying clinician, in the July 9 draft (p. 4-5). On July 9 the board sent the practicum and the didactic hours to the committee together. The July 9 draft also sets 10 hours of mentoring after graduation (p. 6), separate from these totals, a requirement first proposed in the June 12 recommendation (p. 8, origin). The numbers set the cost and time to qualify: see Training hours.

Provide input on this item →

What the July 9 draft does

The June 12 recommendation (pp. 7-8) set 100 hours (facilitators) / 120 hours (practitioners). The July 9 rules draft now restates those hours in the rule itself, unchanged (p. 5), and adds that they must be completed before applying for certification. The reduction concerns raised in committee did not change the numbers.

▸ Show the full record12 quotesThe June 12 recommendation, the committee input and the July 9 didactic-versus-practicum debate, and the July 9 draft language. All verbatim, with page citations.
1The recommendationJune 12, as written
Exhibit · Recommendation, June 12, verbatim
  • "Supervised practice training… must include a minimum of one hundred (100) hours… for Facilitators and one hundred and twenty (120) hours for Practitioners."
  • "Students shall participate in a minimum of eighty (80) hours of administration and same-day therapy sessions… with a minimum of fourteen (14) different patients over a minimum of 8 different… sessions" (≥6 individual with ≥6 patients; ≥2 group with 4+ each).
  • "Students shall participate in a minimum of twenty (20) hours of in-person preparation and post-integration therapy sessions" (≥6 patients; ≥1 group prep and ≥1 group post-integration).
  • "Practitioners must complete an additional minimum of twenty (20) hours as a practitioner supervising facilitators…"
  • "All practicum hours must take place in an approved Healing Center."

Verbatim from the June 12 recommendation, pp. 7-8.

3The rules draftJuly 9, 2026 (7.35.3)
Exhibit · Rules draft, July 9, verbatim

The July 9 draft writes the practicum into the rule itself:

  • "The practicum shall consist of a minimum of 100 hours of supervised practice training for facilitators and 120 hours for practitioners and must be completed prior to applying for certification."
  • "Students shall participate in a minimum of 80 hours of administration and same-day therapy sessions… with a minimum of 14 different patients over a minimum of eight different administration and same-day therapy sessions…"
  • "Practitioners shall complete an additional minimum of 20 hours as a practitioner supervising facilitators…"
  • "All practicum hours shall take place in an approved healing center or other approved location."

Verbatim from the July 9, 2026 rules draft (7.35.3), p. 5: practitioner and facilitator practicum requirements. The hours match the June 12 recommendation.

Where it stands

July 17 meeting: the full Advisory Board voted 7-0 to defer the practicum and didactic hours to the Training and Education Committee to develop updated standards. That committee meets at 1 PM today. Larry Leeman said adequate training is hard without the ability for residents to use psilocybin in training, and said he hoped a legislative fix-it bill would add major depressive disorder as a qualifying condition, which he estimated could roughly triple the number of eligible patients.

July 9 meeting: the board voted to table the practicum and didactic hours and send them to the Training and Education Committee (July 17). Larry Leeman suggested the committee bring in outside experts and named the California Institute of Integral Studies. The board tied the practicum to the didactic hours and to a possible "fix-it" bill allowing training with trainees, plus a cost analysis. Section G still lets the department waive practicum without new rulemaking (p. 6). Two wording changes were discussed at the meeting: "experience, observe or conduct" becoming "and conduct," and "therapy sessions" being removed. Neither change appears yet in the July 9 draft text (p. 5 still reads "experience, observe, or conduct… and therapy sessions"). Detail: the tabled numbers and the July 17 decision.

The reciprocity timelineReciprocity · rules draft 7.35.3

Are the reciprocity deadlines adequate? On July 9 the board extended both the reciprocity waiver and the New Mexico program waiver to December 31, 2027.

What this is: reciprocity lets providers trained in other government-approved programs (for example, Colorado or Oregon) qualify in New Mexico under reduced practicum requirements. These dates set how long the reduced requirements remain available.

Provide input on this item →

What the July 9 meeting decided

The June 12 recommendation has no reciprocity provision. The June 25 draft set two dates. At the July 9 meeting the board extended both deadlines to December 31, 2027: the reciprocity practicum-hours waiver (p. 7) and the New Mexico program waiver (p. 6). Board chair Ian Dunn noted December 31 is a legislative backstop, not the target launch date. This was the one tabled item resolved on July 9.

Where it stands

July 9 meeting: resolved without objection. Both the reciprocity waiver and the New Mexico program waiver were extended to December 31, 2027 (Larry Leeman: cohorts finish around March 2027 and need time to complete practicum). Board chair Ian Dunn said December 31 is a legislative backstop, not the launch target. This was the only tabled item settled on July 9.
