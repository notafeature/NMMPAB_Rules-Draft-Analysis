# Source-Verification Report: New Mexico Medical Cannabis / Controlled Substances Act Claims

## TL;DR
- **CLAIM 1 (cannabis certifying-provider DEA/CS registration requirement): CONTRADICTED as stated at the rule level; only partially supported at the administrative-form level.** Neither the Lynn and Erin Compassionate Use Act nor the 7.34 NMAC rules require a certifying provider to hold a DEA registration or a New Mexico controlled-substance registration (CSR) *number*. They require only that the provider be a "practitioner" — "a person licensed in New Mexico to prescribe and administer drugs that are subject to the Controlled Substances Act." Prescriptive/controlled-substance *authority* (scope of license) is the eligibility gate; a *registration number* is not required by rule. (DOH's online provider application does require a NM CSR number, but no rule spells this out, and no DEA number is required anywhere.)
- **CLAIM 2a (NMSA 1978, § 30-31-12(A)): CONFIRMED AS QUOTED**, with one omitted trailing phrase ("in accordance with its regulations"). "The board" means the Board of Pharmacy per § 30-31-2(C), so the document's "[of pharmacy]" gloss is correct.
- **CLAIM 2b (NMSA 1978, § 30-31-6(D)): CONFIRMED AS QUOTED.** All three quoted fragments match the current statutory text verbatim.

## Key Findings

### CLAIM 1 — Certifying-provider registration requirement
The document's assertion that NM DOH *requires* medical-cannabis certifying providers to hold DEA and/or state controlled-substance *registration* is **not supported by the statute or the rule as codified**. What the law requires is prescriptive authority via licensure — not proof of a DEA/CSR registration number.

- **Statute — NMSA 1978, § 26-2B-3 (Lynn and Erin Compassionate Use Act), definition of "practitioner":** "'practitioner' means a person licensed in New Mexico to prescribe and administer drugs that are subject to the Controlled Substances Act." The subsection *letter* has shifted with amendments: it is **Subsection L** in the current (2023 amendment, HB 292) compilation hosted by NM RLD; it appeared as **Subsection E** in the 2015 codification (HB 466) and as **Subsection S** in the 2020 codification (SB 139). This confirms the report's point that the letter varies by codification year, while the operative text is unchanged.
- **Rule — 7.34.2.7 NMAC, Subsection P(4) (identical parallel language in 7.34.3.7 NMAC):** "'Practitioner' means a person licensed in New Mexico to prescribe and administer drugs that are subject to the Controlled Substances Act, Sections 30-31-1 et seq., NMSA 1978."
- The rule is **silent** on DEA registration and silent on any Board of Pharmacy CSR *number* requirement. Per 7.34.3.10 NMAC, the only credential the certification form must contain is "the practitioner's clinical licensure" — there is no DEA-number or CSR-number field in the rule. DOH's controlled-substance-authority check appears as a DOH *verification power* under 7.34.3.11 NMAC (contacting the New Mexico medical board, board of nursing, board of pharmacy, or other licensing agencies "to verify that the practitioner is licensed to practice and prescribe controlled substances in New Mexico and is in good standing"), not as a provider disclosure duty. The terms "DEA" and "CSR" never appear in the certifying-provider rules.
- **Distinguishing (a) vs. (b):** The rule imposes something between the two framings in the query. It requires professional licensure that *carries* prescribe/administer authority over controlled substances — so it is more than "mere professional licensure" (a nurse or pharmacist lacking prescriptive authority would not qualify), but **less than** an explicit requirement to hold and produce a DEA or state CS *registration certificate*. The eligibility gate is the **scope of the license** (authority to prescribe/administer controlled substances), not possession of a DEA/CSR registration.
- **Administrative nuance (the one place the claim finds partial footing):** The DOH online "Medical Provider Application" (post-April-2023 portal) does require the provider to enter a valid "NM Controlled Substance License Number," and DOH's official instructions state applications "cannot be approved without the proper credentials." But this is a form/portal requirement grounded in the "practitioner" definition, not a codified rule provision — and **no DEA number is required** at any point.
- **Current operative authority:** Medical cannabis certification remains under DOH / 7.34 NMAC even after the 2021 Cannabis Regulation Act moved adult-use/commercial cannabis regulation to the Cannabis Control Division (within RLD). The 7.34.2.7 NMAC definitions were last amended effective 2/22/2022; the Compassionate Use Act definitions were last amended by 2021 (1st S.S.), ch. 4, § 58.

### CLAIM 2a — NMSA 1978, § 30-31-12(A) (Registration requirements)
**CONFIRMED AS QUOTED**, with the note that the document's quotation stops mid-sentence. Current full text of Subsection (A):

> "A. A person who manufactures, distributes or dispenses a controlled substance or who proposes to engage in the manufacture, distribution or dispensing of a controlled substance shall obtain a registration issued by the board in accordance with its regulations."

- The document's quoted version omits the closing phrase "in accordance with its regulations" and inserts "[of pharmacy]" as an editorial gloss after "the board." The gloss is **correct**: NMSA 1978 § 30-31-2(C) provides "'board' means the board of pharmacy."
- **Amendment history:** enacted Laws 1972, ch. 84, § 12; amended 1975 (ch. 346, § 1), 2002 (ch. 55, § 1), and 2009 (ch. 72, § 1). The **2009 amendment** (eff. July 1, 2009) deleted "annually" after "shall obtain" — confirmed against Justia's 2006 codification, which read verbatim "shall obtain **annually** a registration issued by the board in accordance with its regulations." The 2002 amendment removed former sentences relating to annual registration of practitioners by their examining/licensing authorities. No amendments since 2009; the quoted language is current as of the 2025 compilation.

### CLAIM 2b — NMSA 1978, § 30-31-6(D) (Schedule I peyote religious-use exemption)
**CONFIRMED AS QUOTED.** Full current text of Subsection (D):

> "D. the enumeration of peyote as a controlled substance does not apply to the use of peyote in bona fide religious ceremonies by a bona fide religious organization, and members of the organization so using peyote are exempt from registration. Any person who manufactures peyote for or distributes peyote to the organization or its members shall comply with the federal Comprehensive Drug Abuse Prevention and Control Act of 1970 and all other requirements of law;"

- All three fragments the document quotes match verbatim: (1) the exemption "does not apply to ... bona fide religious ceremonies"; (2) "members of the organization so using peyote are exempt from registration"; (3) manufacturers/distributors "shall comply with the federal [Comprehensive Drug Abuse Prevention and Control Act of 1970] and all other requirements of law."
- **Amendment history:** § 30-31-6 has been amended many times (1978, 2005, 2007, 2011, 2017, 2018, 2019, and 2021 (1st S.S.), ch. 4, § 64), but Subsection (D)'s peyote language has been stable and identical across the 2006, 2011, 2021, and 2024 codifications. Note: one bill/version rendering (2012 HB 93) contained a stray typo ("beta-keto-N-methyl-organization") that is an artifact of that document, not part of the codified law — the codified text reads cleanly as quoted above.

## Details

**On the "functional equivalent of a prescription" point (relevant to the downstream analogy):** New Mexico case law holds that under the Compassionate Use Act, the written certification issued by a person licensed to prescribe and administer controlled substances is the "functional equivalent of a prescription." See *Maez v. Riley Industrial*, 2015-NMCA-049, 347 P.3d 732, restating *Vialpando v. Ben's Automotive Services*, 2014-NMCA-084, 331 P.3d 975: "the certification required under the Compassionate Use Act by a person licensed in New Mexico to prescribe and administer controlled substances is the functional equivalent of a prescription." This reinforces that the qualifying credential is prescriptive *licensure/authority*, not possession of a registration certificate — cutting against Claim 1 as literally stated.

**Sourcing quality:** Statutory text was confirmed against Justia's current (2024/2025) codifications, FindLaw (last-updated Jan. 1, 2024), and Cornell LII, cross-checked against the New Mexico Legislature's enacted bill texts (nmlegis.gov) and NM Regulation & Licensing Department–hosted compilations (rld.nm.gov). Rule text was confirmed against the New Mexico State Records Center & Archives (srca.nm.gov, the official NMAC publisher), NM DOH rule PDFs (nmhealth.org), and Cornell LII. The § 30-31-12 amendment history and full subsection text were pulled directly from the current Justia codification page.

## Recommendations
1. **Correct Claim 1 in the downstream legal argument.** If the analysis relies on an analogy that NM medical-cannabis certifiers must hold DEA and/or state CS *registration*, that premise is overstated and should be reframed. Accurate statement: the certifier must be *licensed in New Mexico to prescribe and administer controlled substances* (i.e., hold prescriptive authority within the license's scope), but neither the Compassionate Use Act nor 7.34 NMAC conditions certification on holding or producing a DEA registration; any CSR-number requirement exists only at the DOH portal-form level, not in the codified rule. Do not represent the rule as imposing a DEA/registration gate.
2. **Fix the Claim 2a quotation** by restoring the omitted phrase "in accordance with its regulations." Keep the "[of pharmacy]" bracket — it is substantively correct under § 30-31-2(C).
3. **Claim 2b may be cited as-is.** For maximum precision, quote the full Subsection (D) rather than fragments, and cite a clean codified source (Justia 2024 or FindLaw) rather than a bill version, to avoid importing the 2012-bill typo.
4. **Benchmarks that would change the analysis:** Claim 1 would move toward "supported" only if (a) DOH amends 7.34 NMAC to add an express DEA/CSR-registration requirement for certifiers, or (b) a New Mexico court construes the statutory/rule "practitioner" definition to require registration rather than mere licensure. Monitor the New Mexico Register for 7.34 NMAC amendments and the annual NMSA compilations for § 26-2B-3 changes.

## Caveats
- **Subsection lettering drift:** The "practitioner" definition in § 26-2B-3 has appeared as Subsection E (2015), S (2020), and L (2023/current). Cite the specific codification year to avoid a mismatched subsection letter; the operative text is unchanged across all of them.
- **Operational provisions vs. definitions:** The exact contents of 7.34.3.10 and 7.34.3.11 NMAC (certification-form contents and DOH verification) were confirmed via the official srca.nm.gov rule text and DOH materials; because these operational parts are amended more frequently than the definitions, a reader relying on them should pull the current part text directly from the New Mexico Register / srca.nm.gov.
- **The DOH provider-application CSR-number requirement** is documented in DOH's official Online Patient Portal provider instructions rather than in a numbered rule. Treat it as administrative practice, not a codified mandate — an important distinction if the legal argument turns on what the *rule* (as opposed to the *form*) requires.
- **Distinction to preserve throughout:** "authority to prescribe/administer controlled substances" (a function of license scope, which the statute/rule DO require) is not the same as "holding a DEA or state controlled-substance registration" (which the statute/rule do NOT require). Collapsing these two is the core error in Claim 1 as written.
