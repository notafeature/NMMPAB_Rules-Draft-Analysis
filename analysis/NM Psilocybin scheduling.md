# Psilocybin Scheduling Under New Mexico's SB 219 and the Controlled-Substance-Registration Question for Program Clinicians

## TL;DR
- **Psilocybin and psilocin remain listed in Schedule I of the New Mexico Controlled Substances Act after SB 219; they were NOT moved to Schedule II or any lower schedule.** SB 219 instead inserted a *carve-out* (NMSA 1978, § 30-31-6, new Subsection E) providing that the Schedule I "enumeration of psilocybin and psilocin … does not apply to their medical use as provided in the Medical Psilocybin Act." So under the user's own framing, the "it's Schedule II or lower, therefore maybe no CS number" argument is **moot** — but that does not end the analysis.
- **The schedule is not the controlling variable.** New Mexico's state controlled-substance registration (NMSA 1978, § 30-31-12) and the federal DEA registration both key off the *act* of manufacturing, distributing, dispensing, or prescribing a controlled substance — not merely off a substance's schedule number. Because the psilocybin program is built on an **administer/recommend** model (a clinician "administers" psilocybin in an approved setting and "recommends" the program), not a **prescribe** model, a certifying/qualifying clinician has a strong argument they need **no** controlled-substance registration to participate — the same structural logic that lets NM medical-cannabis providers "certify" without a cannabis prescription.
- **Federal DEA registration is neither needed nor usable for psilocybin.** Psilocybin stays federal Schedule I; a Schedule I drug cannot be prescribed under 21 U.S.C. § 829, and an ordinary practitioner DEA registration cannot authorize handling it. The open questions are program-design details the NM Department of Health has not yet resolved in rule (the 7.35 NMAC series is not yet written): who physically possesses/dispenses the psilocybin, and whether the *producer* or a facility — as opposed to the clinician — must hold a state registration.

## Key Findings

**1. Exact statutory language: Schedule I with a medical-use carve-out.** SB 219 (2025 Regular Session; signed by Governor Michelle Lujan Grisham on April 7, 2025 as Laws 2025, Chapter 73; effective June 20, 2025) amended NMSA 1978, § 30-31-6 (Schedule I). Psilocybin and psilocin remain expressly listed as Schedule I hallucinogens, but each entry was amended to read "except as provided otherwise in the Controlled Substances Act and the Medical Psilocybin Act," and a new Subsection E was added:

> "the enumeration of psilocybin and psilocin in this schedule does not apply to their medical use as provided in the Medical Psilocybin Act. Any person who is a producer or clinician under that act shall comply with the federal Comprehensive Drug Abuse Prevention and Control Act of 1970 and other requirements of law."

This is the same drafting architecture the statute already uses for the peyote religious-use exemption in Subsection D — the substance stays in Schedule I, but the "enumeration … does not apply" to a defined lawful use. It is an exemption/carve-out, **not** a rescheduling to Schedule II–V. The bill passed with strong bipartisan majorities (Senate 33–4; House 56–9), making New Mexico the third state to authorize medical psilocybin and the first to do so by legislation rather than ballot initiative.

**2. The bill title confirms intent — "remove … from the schedule for purposes of qualified medical treatment," not reschedule.** The enacted title reads: "AMENDING THE CONTROLLED SUBSTANCES ACT TO REMOVE PSILOCYBIN AND PSILOCIN FROM THE SCHEDULE FOR PURPOSES OF QUALIFIED MEDICAL TREATMENT." The removal is use-specific, not a wholesale reschedule. Outside the Medical Psilocybin Act, psilocybin/psilocin remain Schedule I contraband under New Mexico law.

**3. A separate 2025 bill that WOULD have tied NM scheduling to federal rescheduling died.** SB 410 (2025), "Crystalline Polymorph Psilocybin Rescheduling," sponsored solely by Sen. Craig W. Brandt (R-40), was introduced February 14, 2025, referred to SHPAC/SJC, and would have added language auto-rescheduling FDA-approved crystalline polymorph psilocybin to match federal law. It was "Tabled Indefinitely, Bill Has Died" on February 19, 2025. So NM has no automatic-reschedule trigger; the only operative change is SB 219's carve-out.

**4. New Mexico's CS registration requirement keys off conduct, not schedule.** NMSA 1978, § 30-31-12(A): "A person who manufactures, distributes or dispenses a controlled substance or who proposes to engage in the manufacture, distribution or dispensing of a controlled substance shall obtain a registration issued by the board [of pharmacy]." The Board of Pharmacy rules (16.19.20 NMAC) implement this for "practitioners … licensed or certified to prescribe and administer" controlled substances. The trigger is the regulated *act*, not the schedule number.

**5. SB 219 uses an "administer/recommend" model, not a "prescribe" model.** The Act defines "clinician" as "an approved health care provider licensed in New Mexico who holds a permit from the department [of health]." A clinician provides "medical services" (preparation, administration, and integration sessions) and the Act protects a clinician who is "administering" psilocybin or "recommending the program … or providing medical services." The word "prescribe" is not used for psilocybin anywhere in the operative provisions. This mirrors the medical-cannabis "certify"/"recommend" model.

**6. The medical-cannabis analogy is direct — and instructive.** In NM's Medical Cannabis Program (Lynn and Erin Compassionate Use Act, 2007), providers "certify"/"recommend"; they do not "prescribe" cannabis (a Schedule I substance federally). NM DOH does require cannabis-certifying providers to be practitioners "licensed to prescribe and administer" controlled substances — i.e., to hold DEA + NM CS registration generally — but that registration is used for *other* (Schedule II–V) drugs; no one obtains or uses a CS registration to "prescribe" cannabis, because that is federally impossible.

**7. Federal DEA registration is schedule-specific and cannot reach psilocybin for clinical use.** Prescription authority under 21 U.S.C. § 829 exists only for Schedules II–V. Schedule I substances have "no currently accepted medical use in treatment in the United States" (21 U.S.C. § 812(b)(1)(B)) and cannot be prescribed. An ordinary practitioner DEA registration (Form 224) cannot authorize handling a Schedule I drug; the only DEA pathway to Schedule I is a separate researcher (Form 225) or manufacturer registration with an approved protocol (21 C.F.R. § 1301.13(e)). *Conant v. Walters*, 309 F.3d 629 (9th Cir. 2002), confirms physicians may lawfully "recommend" (protected First Amendment speech) but not "prescribe" a Schedule I drug, and a recommendation does not use — and cannot cost — a DEA prescribing registration.

**8. Oregon and Colorado both deliberately avoided the prescribe/DEA problem.** Oregon (Measure 109 / ORS 475A) and Colorado (Prop 122 / Natural Medicine Health Act, SB 23-290) both use licensed **facilitators** who **administer** psilocybin at licensed centers; nothing is "prescribed," no DEA registration is used, and the statutes expressly disclaim exempting anyone from federal law. Colorado's program is described as one in which natural medicine is not prescribed or dispensed through traditional medical channels but administered in structured facilitation sessions (a program-level description consistent with the NMHA and DORA Natural Medicine Division rules, rather than a verbatim statutory quote). NM's clinician-administered model is the medicalized cousin of these facilitator models.

## Details

**What SB 219 did and did not do to scheduling.** The user's threshold question — is psilocybin now Schedule II or lower at the state level, or still Schedule I with only a carve-out — has a clear answer: **still Schedule I, with a use-specific carve-out.** The compound was not relocated to Schedule II, III, IV, or V. The enacted text keeps psilocybin at § 30-31-6(C)(14) and psilocin at § 30-31-6(C)(15) — both squarely within Schedule I's hallucinogen list — and simply appends "except as provided otherwise in … the Medical Psilocybin Act," reinforced by the new Subsection E. The mechanism is legally identical to the peyote-for-religious-use exemption already in Subsection D: the drug stays scheduled, but its "enumeration … does not apply" to the sanctioned use.

That means the user's first conditional — "IF the substance is Schedule II or lower, THEN maybe no CS number needed" — is **not satisfied.** Psilocybin is not Schedule II or lower in New Mexico. Under the user's own logic, that particular argument ("no CS number because it's II or lower") is moot and not worth pursuing.

**But the user's second conditional is where the analysis should NOT stop.** The user framed it as: "IF psilocybin is still Schedule I at the state level, regardless of any carve-out, THEN the argument is moot." That framing conflates two different things: the substance's *schedule* and the clinician's *registration obligation*. New Mexico's registration requirement does not turn on the schedule number at all — it turns on whether a person is manufacturing, distributing, dispensing, or prescribing a controlled substance (§ 30-31-12). A carve-out that removes medical-use psilocybin from the schedule entirely (for program purposes) is, if anything, *more* favorable to the "no registration" position than a reschedule to II would be — because a Schedule II drug is fully within the CS registration/prescription regime, whereas carved-out medical psilocybin is treated, for program conduct, as outside the schedules altogether (like religious-use peyote, whose users "are exempt from registration" under § 30-31-6(D)).

**So the real controlling variable is (b): the act the clinician performs, not the schedule.** The correct framing is: a state or federal controlled-substance registration is required to *prescribe/dispense/distribute/manufacture* a controlled substance. It is not required simply to *recommend*, *certify*, *approve*, or *administer within a specialized state program* a substance that has been statutorily exempted for that use. Because SB 219 (i) exempts medical-use psilocybin from the schedule and (ii) casts the clinician's role as administering/recommending rather than prescribing, the strongest reading is that the qualifying/certifying clinician does **not** need a state CS registration *for the psilocybin itself*, and certainly cannot use a DEA registration for it.

**The peyote exemption is the cleanest internal-statute precedent.** Section 30-31-6(D) says peyote's enumeration "does not apply to … bona fide religious ceremonies," and "members of the organization so using peyote are exempt from registration," while anyone who "manufactures … or distributes peyote … shall comply with the federal [CSA] and all other requirements of law." SB 219's new Subsection E is a near-verbatim structural parallel for psilocybin producers and clinicians. The drafters plainly modeled the psilocybin carve-out on the peyote exemption — strong textual evidence that participants are meant to operate *outside* the ordinary registration regime for the exempted conduct, while the "comply with federal law" clause preserves the federal overhang.

**The medical-cannabis structural analogy.** NM has run a "certify not prescribe" controlled-substance program since 2007. Providers issue a written certification that a patient has a qualifying condition; the patient then buys cannabis from a licensed dispensary. No provider writes a cannabis "prescription," and no provider uses a DEA number for cannabis (they can't — it's federal Schedule I). NM DOH nonetheless requires certifying providers to be practitioners who are generally "licensed to prescribe and administer" controlled substances — meaning they hold the ordinary DEA + state CS credentials used for their *other* prescribing. That is a licensure-eligibility gate, not a psilocybin/cannabis-specific registration. SB 219's psilocybin program can (and appears designed to) follow the same template: the clinician's baseline prescriptive authority may be an *eligibility criterion* set by DOH rule, but no CS registration is *exercised* on the psilocybin.

**Federal overlay: the DEA problem is avoided by design, not solved.** Nothing in state law can lift the federal Schedule I status of psilocybin. The federal system offers no route for a clinician to "prescribe" it, and an ordinary DEA registration is useless for it. The only DEA registration reaching Schedule I is a research or manufacturer registration with an approved protocol — relevant to producers/researchers, not to certifying clinicians. This is precisely why Oregon, Colorado, and now New Mexico all avoid the word "prescribe" and instead use "administer"/"recommend"/"facilitate." SB 219's "comply with the federal … Act" clause is a reminder that federal law still applies (and that participants act at some residual federal risk), not a grant of any federal registration. Notably, SB 219 also *excludes FDA-approved psilocybin products* from the program (permitting them only in specified research and equity-funded treatment scenarios), so the program deliberately runs on non-FDA-approved, naturally occurring psilocybin — reinforcing that it operates outside the ordinary prescribe/dispense pharmacy channel.

**What remains genuinely open in New Mexico.** SB 219 is a framework statute; the operative rules have not been written. The DOH Center for Medical Cannabis and Psilocybin, directed by Dominick Zurlo, is in active rulemaking. At the Medical Psilocybin Advisory Board's first public meeting on December 5, 2025, Zurlo said the department has "set a goal now to … be able to see the initial patients by the end of December of 2026" — an accelerated target ahead of the statutory December 31, 2027 full-implementation deadline. The psilocybin rules (expected in the 7.35 NMAC series, paralleling the 7.34 NMAC medical-cannabis rules) do not yet exist. As a result:
- The Act itself does **not** state a schedule for program psilocybin beyond the carve-out, and does **not** expressly say whether clinicians need a controlled-substance registration. That silence will be filled by DOH rule.
- It is not yet settled who holds/dispenses the psilocybin: the "producer" grows and prepares it; the "clinician" administers it. Whether a *facility* or *producer* must hold a NM Board of Pharmacy facility registration (given the peyote/cannabis distribution overhang and § 30-31-12) is unresolved, even though the individual certifying clinician likely does not.
- DOH could, as a matter of policy (mirroring cannabis), require participating clinicians to hold general DEA + NM CS registration as an eligibility credential — even though that registration would not be used to handle psilocybin. Watch the forthcoming rules for this.

## Recommendations

**For defining program terms now (pre-rule):**
1. **Drop the "Schedule II or lower" argument.** It is factually wrong for New Mexico — psilocybin is still state Schedule I with a carve-out — so any theory premised on a II-or-lower classification should be abandoned. Do not build program terms on it.
2. **Re-anchor the analysis on prescribe-vs-certify, not on the schedule.** Adopt the position that a certifying/qualifying clinician needs **no controlled-substance registration to certify/recommend/approve** a patient for psilocybin, because (a) SB 219 exempts medical-use psilocybin from the schedule for program conduct, and (b) the clinician does not prescribe or dispense a controlled substance — the trigger under § 30-31-12. Document the peyote-exemption (§ 30-31-6(D)) and medical-cannabis "certify" precedents as support.
3. **Distinguish state vs. federal registration explicitly in your program terms.** State: no NM CS registration is exercised on psilocybin. Federal: no DEA registration is used or usable for psilocybin (Schedule I; 21 U.S.C. § 829; *Conant v. Walters*). If clinicians hold DEA/state CS numbers, they hold them for their ordinary practice, not for psilocybin.
4. **Separate the clinician question from the producer/facility question.** Even if the clinician needs no registration, resolve whether the producer or treatment facility must register with the Board of Pharmacy for possession/handling, given § 30-31-12 and the "comply with federal law" clause. This is the more likely locus of any registration requirement.

**Staged next steps and the benchmarks that change them:**
5. **Monitor DOH rulemaking (7.35 NMAC).** If DOH's rules make general DEA/NM CS registration an *eligibility* criterion for clinicians (as the cannabis program effectively does), incorporate that as a credentialing gate — but keep the terms clear that the registration is not used to handle psilocybin. **Benchmark:** publication of proposed clinician-permit rules (expected during the 2026 rulemaking cycle).
6. **Re-open the schedule question only if two triggers occur:** (a) FDA approval + DEA rescheduling of a psilocybin product below Schedule I (which would change the analysis for that *FDA-approved product* — but note SB 219 *excludes* FDA-approved psilocybin products from the program); or (b) a future NM amendment actually rescheduling psilocybin (note SB 410, the vehicle for federal-conformity auto-rescheduling, died in 2025). Absent these, the state Schedule I status holds.
7. **If precision is required, seek a Board of Pharmacy / DOH advisory or NM Attorney General opinion** on whether program clinicians and producers must register under § 30-31-12. This is the definitive way to resolve the residual ambiguity before the program launches.

## Caveats
- **Rules not yet written.** The controlling operational details will live in forthcoming DOH rules that do not yet exist. Any definitive "no registration needed" statement should be flagged as based on the statute as enacted and subject to change in rulemaking.
- **Federal risk persists.** State-law exemption does not eliminate federal Schedule I exposure; SB 219 itself directs producers and clinicians to "comply with the federal … Act." Participants act with residual federal legal risk that no state registration analysis can cure.
- **"Clinician" ≠ "prescriber" in this Act.** SB 219's defined "clinician" is a DOH-permitted health care provider; the Act does not itself require that person to hold prescriptive/CS authority. Whether DOH layers on such a requirement is a policy choice to watch.
- **Timeline fluidity.** The SB 219 statutory full-implementation deadline is December 31, 2027; DOH (via Director Dominick Zurlo, and confirmed by DOH spokesperson David Barre to the Santa Fe New Mexican) is targeting initial patients by December 2026, a date "moved forward because there has been a lot of interest for the program." Some secondary sources erroneously state December 31, 2026 or January 2028; treat the December 31, 2027 statutory deadline as controlling and the December 2026 figure as an accelerated agency goal.
- **This is analysis, not legal advice.** For a program-defining decision, confirm with NM Board of Pharmacy and DOH counsel, ideally in writing.
