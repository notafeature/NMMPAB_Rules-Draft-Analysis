# The Controlled Substance Number Access Point: A Cited Reference

**Subject:** The draft New Mexico Medical Psilocybin Program rule (7.35.3) requiring the "certifying clinician" (renamed from "diagnostic clinician") who authorizes a patient's entry to hold a New Mexico Controlled Substance number.

**Purpose:** Neutral reference material for a community website. Educate on the concept, assemble use cases, and record expert positions. This document takes no position on whether the requirement should stay, change, or be removed. Every factual claim carries a source. Where a source is a named person, the position is theirs, not this document's.

**Status of the requirement (as of July 9, 2026):** The requirement is in the department's written July 9 draft. At the July 9, 2026 Advisory Board meeting the board did not endorse it and sent it back to the department; the rename "diagnostic clinician" to "certifying clinician" was confirmed. The verbatim board discussion is in Section 9. The operative rule is not final; the statute delegates clinician qualifications to Department of Health (DOH) rulemaking still underway.

**How to read the citations:** Findings in Sections 1 through 4 were run through the research harness's adversarial verification (three independent reviewers per claim; a claim survived only if not refuted). Findings in Sections 5 through 8 were extracted verbatim from primary and secondary sources but were not put through the same three-vote verification, so they are labeled by source quality and flagged where a source is out-of-state or secondary. Section 9 is the local July 9 transcript.

---

## 1. What a New Mexico Controlled Substance number is

**It is a state registration issued by the New Mexico Board of Pharmacy, separate from a federal DEA number.**

- The registration is created by the New Mexico Controlled Substances Act (NMSA 1978, Sections 30-31-1 through 30-31-42) and the Board of Pharmacy rule 16.19.20 NMAC. Rule 16.19.20.3 NMAC states that "Section 30-31-11 of the Controlled Substances Act, 30-31-1 through 30-31-42 NMSA 1978, authorizes the board of pharmacy to promulgate regulations for registration and control of controlled substances." The issuing agency is the New Mexico Board of Pharmacy, within the Regulation and Licensing Department (RLD), 5500 San Antonio Drive NE Suite C, Albuquerque, NM 87109. *(Verified 3-0. Sources: 16.19.20 NMAC; RLD Board of Pharmacy forms page; RLD practitioner application form.)*

- It is required of any person or entity that manufactures, distributes, dispenses, administers, prescribes, delivers, analyzes, or conducts research using controlled substances in the state. Rule 16.19.20.8 NMAC enumerates the required registrant categories. *(Verified 3-0. Source: 16.19.20 NMAC.)*

- **Who may hold it:** eligibility is limited to enumerated prescribing/administering license types. The Controlled Substances Act definition (NMSA 30-31-2(P)) reads: "'practitioner' means a physician, certified advanced practice chiropractic physician, doctor of oriental medicine, dentist, physician assistant, certified nurse practitioner, clinical nurse specialist, certified nurse-midwife, prescribing psychologist, veterinarian, euthanasia technician, pharmacist, pharmacist clinician or other person licensed or certified to prescribe and administer drugs that are subject to the Controlled Substances Act." The closing catch-all ("or other person licensed or certified to prescribe and administer drugs") bounds eligibility to prescribing/administering authority. *(Verified 3-0 for the rule 16.19.20.8 list; the statutory 30-31-2(P) quote is from a secondary codification, law.justia.com.)*

- **What it authorizes:** to administer means "the direct application of a controlled substance by any means to the body of a patient... by a practitioner or the practitioner's agent" (NMSA 30-31-2(A)). A "controlled substance" is a drug listed in Schedules I through V (NMSA 30-31-2(E)). The scope of the authority is tied to a "valid practitioner-patient relationship," which the statute defines as "a professional relationship, as defined by the practitioner's licensing board, between the practitioner and the patient" (NMSA 30-31-2(X)). *(Source: law.justia.com codification of NMSA 30-31-2; secondary.)*

- **Difference from a federal DEA registration:** the two are separate and stacked. Rule 16.19.20.14(B) NMAC requires each application to include a current DEA registration and professional license. RLD guidance states the state registration is required before the DEA will issue the federal controlled-substance registration (consistent with 21 CFR Part 1301, which conditions federal DEA registration on state authority to dispense). Both must be in place before a practitioner can enroll in the New Mexico Prescription Monitoring Program. *(Verified 3-0. Sources: 16.19.20 NMAC; RLD practitioner packet; RLD forms page.)*

- **Naming note for the site:** the draft rule requires the **New Mexico** state Controlled Substance number, not the federal DEA number. This distinction was clarified on the record June 26.

## 2. Scope of practice: who can diagnose the qualifying conditions vs. who needs a Controlled Substance number

**A licensed clinical social worker (LCSW) or a licensed psychologist can diagnose PTSD and depression in New Mexico without holding any controlled-substance registration. Diagnosis and prescribing authority are separate under New Mexico law.**

- **Social workers.** The Social Work Practice Act (NMSA 1978, Section 61-31-6) defines clinical social work practice as "the professional application of social work theory and methods in the diagnosis, treatment and prevention of psychosocial dysfunction, disability or impairment, including but not limited to emotional and mental disorders," and defines clinical social work services as "assessment, diagnosis and treatment, including psychotherapy and counseling." The section contains no reference to controlled-substance registration, DEA number, or prescribing authority. LCSWs have no prescriptive authority in New Mexico. *(Verified 3-0. Source: NMSA 61-31-6.)*

- **Psychologists and the prescribing (RxE) distinction.** An ordinary licensed psychologist may diagnose and treat mental disorders but has no prescribing authority. New Mexico separately created a "prescribing psychologist" designation and a "conditional prescription certificate": "A prescribing psychologist or a psychologist with a conditional prescription certificate may administer and prescribe psychotropic medication within the recognized scope of the profession... for the treatment of mental disorders." That prescribing subset "must comply with all other state and federal laws regulating the administering and prescribing of controlled substances." The "prescribing psychologist" category was added to the Controlled Substances Act "practitioner" definition effective July 1, 2002. A licensed psychologist without the RxE certificate therefore can diagnose but cannot hold a controlled-substance registration as a prescriber. *(Sources: NMSA 61-9-17.2, secondary codification; NMSA 30-31-2 history note.)*

- **The structural point.** Diagnosing a qualifying condition (PTSD, depression) sits within the scope of several non-prescribing license types. Holding a controlled-substance number is limited to the prescribing/administering license types in Section 1. The draft rule 7.35.3 requirement therefore narrows the certifying pool from "anyone who can diagnose the condition" to "a subset who can also prescribe controlled substances." *(This is a description of the two cited scopes, not a position on the rule.)*

- **Open question the research did not resolve:** whether DOH's final rule will accept a non-prescribing provider (LCSW, or a psychologist without RxE) as a certifying clinician. The scope statutes establish that diagnosis does not require a Controlled Substance number; they do not decide what DOH's psilocybin rule will require. *(Flagged in the harness caveats.)*

## 3. What the Medical Psilocybin Act says about "medical appropriateness" and eligibility

**Entry is not based on a qualifying-condition diagnosis alone. The Act sets a two-part test: a diagnosed qualifying condition AND a separate clinician judgment of medical appropriateness. The Act does not name a license type or require a controlled-substance number in its definition of "clinician."**

- **The program and its date.** The New Mexico Medical Psilocybin Program was created by Senate Bill 219 (the Medical Psilocybin Act), signed into law in April 2025, effective June 20, 2025. *(Verified 3-0. Sources: NM DOH program page; NM Legislature SB 219.)*

- **Qualifying conditions.** SB 219 Section 3(I) enumerates: "(1) major treatment-resistant depression; (2) posttraumatic stress disorder; (3) substance use disorders; (4) end-of-life care; and (5) other conditions approved by the department." Section 8(B) lets the Advisory Board recommend and the Department approve additional conditions. This is a floor the Department may expand. *(Verified 3-0. Sources: SB 219 HTML and PDF; NM DOH.)*

- **The two-part eligibility test.** SB 219 defines a "qualified patient" as a patient "whose clinician has judged the patient to be a medically appropriate candidate for the use of medical psilocybin based on being diagnosed with a qualifying condition." The verb "has judged," together with "medically appropriate candidate," denotes an active clinical determination. Diagnosis is necessary but, on the statute's face, not sufficient by itself; the clinician must also judge appropriateness. *(Verified 3-0. Source: SB 219.)*

- **Definition of "clinician."** SB 219 defines "clinician" as "an approved health care provider licensed in New Mexico who holds a permit from the department to provide medical services to qualified patients." No controlled-substance registration, and no specific license type, appears in that definition. The bill's only controlled-substance references are in the schedule-amendment provisions; its only "prescribe" references are in a gross-receipts-tax section. Clinician qualifications are delegated to DOH rulemaking, which is where draft rule 7.35.3 and the "certifying clinician" term live. *(Verified 3-0. Source: SB 219.)*

- **Scheduling.** SB 219 amends the Controlled Substances Act to carve psilocybin and psilocin out of Schedule I for medical-psilocybin purposes rather than fully de-scheduling them; they remain scheduled for non-medical purposes. *(Verified 2-1, medium confidence. Source: SB 219 PDF. This was the only non-unanimous verified claim.)*

**Reading of Sections 2 and 3 together:** the statute ties "medical appropriateness" to a clinician's judgment layered on top of a qualifying-condition diagnosis, but leaves the license type of that clinician to DOH. The controlled-substance-number requirement is a rulemaking choice by the department, not a directive written into the Act.

## 4. How the draft rule frames the access point

From the July 9 rules draft and the meeting record (local primary sources; see Section 9 and HANDOFF.md):

- The department renamed "diagnostic clinician" to "certifying clinician" throughout the proposed rules after stakeholders said "diagnostic clinician" "didn't cover everything."
- The certifying-clinician application requires, among other items, "certificate or verification of results of their New Mexico controlled substance number."
- The department's stated rationale, read into the record July 9: licenses do have the ability to diagnose some of the conditions ("a social worker can diagnose with regard to PTSD and depression, for example"), "but the main reason for the controlled substance number is because it is a treatment that is occurring with a medication... with it being a medical program, that is part of what ensures that there is that medical evaluation, that medical certification." *(Source: NMMPAB July 9, 2026 transcript, department reader. See Section 9.)*

## 5. How Oregon, Colorado, and Washington DC handle the access point

**None of the three comparator jurisdictions gates access through a clinician who holds prescribing authority. Oregon and Colorado require no diagnosis or qualifying condition at all. DC medical cannabis lets adults self-certify.**

### Oregon (Measure 109 / ORS 475A; Oregon Health Authority)

- The Oregon Health Authority "Access Psilocybin Services" page states, verbatim: "To access psilocybin services, an individual must be 21 years of age or older" and "A prescription or medical referral is not required." *(Verified 3-0. Source: oregon.gov OHA psilocybin access page, primary.)*
- ORS 475A imposes no qualifying-condition gate. Safety contraindications (for example active psychosis, or lithium use within a set window) can disqualify a client during preparation, but these operate as disqualifiers, not as an affirmative diagnosis requirement. *(Verified 3-0.)*

### Colorado (Proposition 122 / SB23-290; Natural Medicine)

- SB23-290 (implementing Prop 122) contains no diagnosis, qualifying-condition, or physician/clinician-gatekeeper requirement for access. Access is gated by age: "A person who is under 21 years of age who knowingly possesses or consumes natural medicine... commits a drug petty offense." The regulated model runs through licensed facilitators and licensed healing centers overseen by the Division of Professions and Occupations and the Natural Medicine Division, not through a medical prescriber. SB23-290 was signed May 23, 2023, effective July 1, 2023. *(Verified 3-0, but rated medium confidence: the "no diagnosis requirement" finding rests partly on the bill summary's silence, corroborated by secondary legal analyses. Source: leg.colorado.gov SB23-290, primary.)*

### Washington DC (medical cannabis self-certification)

- D.C. Code Section 7-1671.05(b)(3) establishes a self-certification form for patients 21 and older containing the attestation: "I will only use cannabis purchased from a District retailer as a qualifying patient for the treatment of a qualifying medical or dental condition." *(Verified 3-0. Source: code.dccouncil.gov, primary.)*
- D.C. Law 24-187 permits individuals 21 and older to "self-certify" on a form provided by the alcohol/cannabis regulator that they are using cannabis for medical purposes, creating an exception to the prior requirement of a signed written practitioner recommendation. *(Verified 3-0. Source: code.dccouncil.gov, primary.)*
- Caveats for the site: minors still require a practitioner recommendation; Law 24-187 was temporary and was later made permanent by D.C. Law 24-332 (Medical Cannabis Amendment Act of 2022); the agency formerly ABRA is now ABCA.

**Contrast.** New Mexico's draft model is clinician-gated (a diagnosed qualifying condition plus a certifying clinician's appropriateness judgment). Oregon and Colorado use no clinical gate beyond age and a safety screen; DC medical cannabis uses patient self-certification. New Mexico's proposed access point is the most restrictive of the four.

## 6. Consultation-model and telemedicine-referral precedents

These bear on the alternatives floated July 9 (consultation model; behavioral-health plus telemedicine referral). Sources are primary federal but were not put through three-vote verification.

**Ryan Haight Act baseline.** The Ryan Haight Online Pharmacy Consumer Protection Act of 2008 governs online prescribing of controlled substances, is enforced by the DEA, and requires a practitioner to conduct at least one in-person medical evaluation before remotely prescribing a controlled substance, subject to specified exemptions. After one in-person evaluation, the practitioner may prescribe via telemedicine thereafter; the Act does not mandate the frequency of further in-person visits. *(Source: APA Ryan Haight toolkit, primary.)*

**Covering-practitioner exception.** The Act contains a "covering practitioner" exception: a practitioner may conduct a telemedicine evaluation "at the request of a practitioner who... has conducted at least 1 in-person medical evaluation of the patient or an evaluation of the patient through the practice of telemedicine within the previous 24 months." It also contains exceptions for federal health systems (Indian Health Service; Department of Veterans Affairs). *(Source: APA Ryan Haight toolkit, primary.)* This is a structural precedent for one clinician relying on another clinician's prior evaluation.

**Post-COVID flexibilities.** The DEA, jointly with HHS, issued a fourth extension of COVID-19 telemedicine flexibilities for prescribing controlled substances, effective January 1, 2026 through December 31, 2026. Under the extension, a DEA-registered practitioner may prescribe Schedule II-V controlled substances via telemedicine "without having conducted an in-person medical evaluation," provided the prescription is "issued for a legitimate medical purpose by a practitioner acting in the usual course of professional practice" and complies with 21 CFR Part 1306. *(Sources: Federal Register fourth-extension rule; APA toolkit, both primary.)*

**Proposed permanent framework.** The DEA has proposed a "special registration" framework that would let a patient receive prescribed controlled medications through telemedicine "without ever having an in-person medical evaluation." The special registration would cover Schedule III-V prescribing; a separate Advanced Telemedicine Prescribing Registration for Schedule II is limited to board-certified psychiatrists, hospice physicians, long-term-care physicians, and pediatricians. The proposal would also, for the first time, require online platforms that connect patients and prescribers to register with DEA, and would establish a national PDMP. *(Source: DEA telemedicine rulemaking materials, primary. Note: proposed, not final.)*

**Reading for the site:** existing federal controlled-substance law already recognizes (a) one clinician relying on another's prior evaluation (covering-practitioner exception) and (b) telemedicine prescribing without a prior in-person visit under the current flexibilities. These are precedents for the consultation and telemedicine-referral alternatives, not endorsements of them, and they concern prescribing, which differs from the psilocybin program's "certify for entry" act.

## 7. Malpractice and liability framework for certifying a patient one will not treat

This addresses the liability concern that dominated the July 9 discussion. The controlling illustrations are out-of-state case law (Minnesota) applied here only to describe the general framework, not New Mexico law. Sources are secondary. Treat as an educational framing of how liability can attach, not as a statement of New Mexico doctrine.

**General rule.** "Generally, a physician owes no duty to a patient without a physician-patient relationship. Without a physician-patient relationship, with no duty owed, there is no liability for 'breach' of that nonexistent duty." *(Source: National Legal Research Group, secondary.)*

**The exception that matters here.** Courts have found liability without a formal physician-patient relationship where a physician makes a formal medical decision that a third party foreseeably relies on. In Warren v. Dinter (Minnesota Supreme Court), the court held: "a duty arises between a physician and an identified third party when the physician provides medical advice and it is foreseeable that the third party will rely on that advice." The case involved a hospitalist who, by phone, declined to admit a patient he never examined, based on a nurse practitioner's presentation; the court found liability possible because "the patient was reliant on the hospitalist's decision." *(Sources: Warren v. Dinter as reported by Medical Economics and secondary summaries.)*

**The formal-decision line.** The Dinter court distinguished a formal medical decision from an informal "curbside" consult: the "decision today should not be misinterpreted as being about informal advice from one medical professional to another. This case is about a formal medical decision." *(Source: Medical Economics, secondary.)*

**Supervisory relationship.** A supervising physician can face liability for a patient he never examined based on a contractual/supervisory relationship with the provider who did see the patient. *(Source: Medical Economics, secondary.)*

**Reading for the site:** the general framework is that a certifying clinician's formal decision, if a patient foreseeably relies on it, can create a duty even without hands-on treatment, while informal advice is treated differently. This describes why a certifying-clinician role can carry liability exposure. It does not quantify New Mexico exposure, and no New Mexico-specific certifying-clinician liability authority was located. Whether the psilocybin certification is a "formal medical decision" of the kind that attaches a duty is an unlitigated question.

## 8. New Mexico rural access and prescriber-shortage data

Sources are a mix of primary federal (HHS OIG; University of Washington rural health research) and secondary (Commonwealth Fund reporting; KFF). Not three-vote verified.

**HPSA designations.**
- "27 of New Mexico's 33 counties are designated in part or whole as health care professional shortage areas." *(Commonwealth Fund, "The State of Rural Primary Care in the United States," November 2025, reported by NM In Depth; secondary.)*
- "Almost all of New Mexico's counties have a 'health professional shortage area' for mental health." *(HHS Office of Inspector General, OEI-02-17-00490, primary.)*
- Historical baseline: per HRSA data cited for 2013, "only one of New Mexico's 33 counties, Los Alamos, had enough providers to adequately care for its population." *(NM In Depth, secondary.)*

**Prescriber scarcity, especially rural.**
- "Ten frontier counties, with a total of 27,000 Medicaid managed care enrollees, have no [behavioral health] prescribers. Rural and frontier counties have a median of 0.2 prescribers and 0.0 prescribers per 1,000 Medicaid managed care enrollees, while urban counties have a median of 0.7." *(HHS OIG OEI-02-17-00490, primary.)*
- "19 of the State's 33 counties have fewer than 2 licensed behavioral health providers for every 1,000 Medicaid managed care enrollees. All 19... are rural or frontier," including 3 counties with no providers at all. *(HHS OIG, primary.)*
- "Only 29 percent of licensed [behavioral health] providers are located in rural and frontier counties, even though nearly half of the State's Medicaid managed care enrollees reside in these counties." *(HHS OIG, primary.)*

**Psychiatrists vs. non-prescribing clinicians (the prescriber gap).**
- Rural New Mexico had 40 psychiatrists total, 5.6 per 100,000, versus 21.6 per 100,000 (306) in urban counties. Statewide, 16.2 per 100,000 (346), below the U.S. rate of 17.7. *(University of Washington Rural Health Research Center, June 2025 NM behavioral health data brief, primary.)*
- Rural New Mexico had 55 psychiatric nurse practitioners (7.7 per 100,000), roughly half the urban rate of 14.9. *(UW RHRC, primary.)*
- By contrast, non-prescribing clinicians are far more numerous rurally: social workers reached 140.0 per 100,000 (997) in rural New Mexico, against 5.6 per 100,000 for psychiatrists. *(UW RHRC, primary.)*
- Multiple rural counties (including Rio Arriba, San Juan, Doña Ana, Chaves, Eddy, Catron, Cibola, Sierra) had zero psychiatrists per 100,000 in 2025. *(UW RHRC, primary.)*

**General physician shortage.**
- "In July [2023]... the state was short 1,000 physicians and almost 7,000 nurses, according to published job announcements." *(NM In Depth, secondary.)*
- About a third of New Mexico's population, estimated between 540,000 and 800,000 people, lives in rural and frontier areas, in a state of 121,000 square miles with 2.1 million residents. *(NM In Depth, secondary.)*

**Reading for the site:** the data show prescriber-eligible clinicians are the scarcest category and are concentrated outside rural counties, while non-prescribing clinicians who can diagnose the qualifying conditions are far more available rurally. This is the empirical backdrop to the rural-access concern raised by board members July 9; it is data, not a position.

## 9. The July 9, 2026 board discussion, verbatim by speaker

Source: NMMPAB July 9, 2026 unofficial transcript (`docs/documents/NMMPAB-2026-07-09-transcript.pdf`), speakers inferred. Names corrected per HANDOFF (Peskuski, Zurlo). Outcome: the certifying-clinician / CS-number item was sent back to the department; no vote was taken. Ian Dunn will take it back to the department to reconsider.

**Department reader (rationale for the requirement):**
> "The difference being that with the clinician, the certifying clinician is the New Mexico controlled substance number. And I know there's been a lot of question about that, about why we are requiring that. Licenses have the ability to be able to diagnose some of the conditions. And yes, that is true. For example, a social worker can diagnose with regard to PTSD and depression, for example. But the main reason for the controlled substance number is because it is a treatment that is occurring with a medication. And so with that... the certifying individual, in this case, the clinician, does need to have a controlled substance number... with it being a medical program, that is part of what ensures that there is that medical evaluation, that medical certification."

**Chris Peskuski (liability, access, program sustainability; proposes a consultation model):**
> "Some of the liability issues, I think, with this... I'm concerned that putting the access point through a doctor is going to make it very risky for clinicians to certify anybody into this program because they're going to be subject to malpractice. I believe... all the liability should lie in the room where the patient's being treated because ultimately all the risk mitigation comes from proper preparation, administration techniques and integration techniques. And... why would a clinician sign off on a patient when he's not gonna be necessarily in the room when all of that happens[?]"
> "The access issues for this are... very troubling. My wife hasn't been able to get a primary care practitioner for over a year now and we live in Albuquerque and she's got great insurance through Sandia Labs. The access outside of Bernalillo County is going to be... unattainable."
> "I think with the huge shortage of primary care, or people with these licenses in New Mexico, coupled with the amount of risk and the training that they have to take to enter the program, it's just going to make this completely inaccessible... I would recommend that we look towards a model of consultation... where the practitioners are required to consult... to determine medical readiness in circumstances where that's necessary and allows for continuity of care for practitioners to work with doctors that are already treating patients. And I think it solves a lot of these issues of liability and accessibility."

**Dan Jennings (rural access; in-person requirement; end-of-life equity):**
> "I got to really lift up what Chris said. From rural area, my big concern... is that I would have to find this person who is a doctor, who has a CS, who has gone through the program, and I physically have to travel to them... I would have to find that person who was going to be in Albuquerque or Santa Fe... but it won't be here. And I could not do it via tele. The only time I could is if I get recertified... if you're a patient that is an end-of-life, that means that end-of-life patient must go to that physician... So, if my mom who died of cancer would need to get this, she would physically not be able to be in this program."

**Ian Dunn, chair (DC self-certification model; contraindication list; existential distress):**
> "In a rare turn of events, I'm going to side with them on the grounds that upon doing my evaluations, psilocybin appears very safe and one of the models I was comparing it to was the Washington DC medical cannabis model which allows you to self-certify. So what if we could provide them with a list of contraindications, say, 'Hey, you got any of this?' If they say no, then we can move them on."
> "I would love to have everyone have access to someone with the New Mexico controlled substances number[;] in an ideal world everyone would have access to healthcare, but unfortunately this is the real world... even some of the conditions that my committee in patient qualification and safety has been looking at was existential distress associated with a life limiting illness. That's not in the DSM-5. That doesn't need to be vetted... by someone with diagnostic[ authority]. I could self-certify."

**Brenda Burgard (difficulty finding prescribers; Adderall bottleneck analogy):**
> "I too agree with Ian on this point and everyone else on the panel here that... this whole week was me trying to find doctors and psychiatrists for my patients currently. For example, for Adderall, the hoops that people have to jump through just to get the medical clearance and then try to find a psychiatrist to prescribe because doctors don't want to prescribe Adderall... I see my client, we're looking at anywhere between three and six months of this type of bottleneck... As Ian said, this is the real world and I think that we really need to think all of this again."

**Keenan Ryan (behavioral-health plus telemedicine referral compromise):**
> "Just throwing out some possibilities, is it possible to [allow] all behavioral health providers and allow through telemedicine? Would that be a meaningful compromise, to allow some sort of recommendation or referral without being restrictive[?]... Some therapists may not have a controlled substance because they're not prescribing, or they might be a psychologist without it, but they could still say that you have PTSD, for example, that would be within their scope and could refer. So just throwing out some options along this spectrum between complete in-person controlled substance versus... all self-certified. I think there could be a happy medium in there."

**Dominic Zurlo (department, summarizing the outcome):**
> "What's the final action on the certifying clinician? So Ian will take that back to here in the department to reconsider."
