## TASK (as received): Update the Training & Education Rules Analysis (EOL layer)

Layer in findings from the July 16, 2026 New Mexico EOL Psilocybin Committee meeting
(Curriculum & Training Review) plus Jen's proposed EOL training deck. Read the current
document first; do not duplicate or disrupt existing structure (existing RACI and EACC
crosswalk hubs and a Committee crosswalk document: extend, do not fork).

### Terminology conventions
- "resident" = the learner/practicum role (not "trainee").
- "Space Attendant" = the second, non-licensed presence in the room (not "space holder"),
  e.g. a hospice volunteer with ~50 hrs training.

### The doc's spine (three-role model)
NM regulation recognizes three roles only: Clinician (diagnostic, licensed); Practitioner
(licensed behavioral health, supervises); Guide / Facilitator (trained, non-licensed).
Everything maps to one of these. There is no palliative/EOL license slot and one should not
be created. Known gap: T&E lists spiritual-care providers (chaplains/doulas) as residents,
but DACPC gives them no post-training slot, and in EOL that unassigned continuity role
becomes load-bearing. Also relevant: the PQS four support tiers (standard / enhanced
clinical / specialized / not appropriate).

### Framing finding
EOL is not a niche condition; it is a stress test that exposes unresolved questions in the
whole framework first and most sharply (fragmented care, the non-directive stance,
two-licensed-provider cost, who owns spiritual care, acuity matching). EOL edits are the
forcing function to resolve program-wide gaps, not a bolt-on section.

### Finding 1: Regulatory vehicle: endorsement, not licensure
DOH (Dom) will not create diagnosis-specific licensure or a required EOL certification
carve-out. Reason: a group session mixing qualifying conditions would force every
facilitator to hold every condition's cert, unworkable. DOH's vehicle is endorsements plus
best-practice recommendations ("can even put that in the best practices"). Denali asked
directly about an EOL certification with a tied requirement; DOH said no, not the preferred
direction. Larry's long-term answer: a professional organization for NM EOL psychedelic
practice should own the mandate, not government. Record EOL as a specialization endorsement
that overlays existing roles, phased by role ("yes, all three domains, phased by role").

### Finding 2: Baseline vs. specialization split (the extraction rule)
Teach the psychospiritual core to everyone; gate the medical/prognostic pieces behind the
EOL specialization.
- Baseline / universal (all facilitators): client mindset and mortality awareness;
  existential/spiritual assessment; grief and loss; informed consent that names the
  possibility of dark/difficult material; ethics and cultural humility; practitioner
  self-care and supervision; and the capacity to hold non-directive presence (see Finding
  5). Backers: Jamie Beachy (existential/spiritual/death themes are ever-present in all
  psilocybin work, e.g. PTSD clients experiencing fear of death), Larry, Greg.
- EOL-specific / specialization overlay: pharmacology and safety in medically fragile/dying
  bodies; symptom-burden and frailty judgment; prognosis / "too close to death" decisions;
  hospice-palliative interface; caregiver-as-co-patient dynamics. Backers: Christine (EOL
  clients have a unique mindset requiring a unique practice/training set), Michael.
This split resolves the Larry-vs-Christine tension: most of what Larry wants in core is the
universal layer; most of what Christine wants adjunct is the medical/prognostic layer.

### Finding 3: Hours, tiers, and the readiness gap
Concrete draft baselines (from Greg):
- Facilitator: 25 hrs shared-therapy + 5 hrs simulated patient + 5 hrs facilitator = 35
  didactic + 100 practicum.
- Practitioner: 35 didactic + 120 practicum. (Role delta is roughly practicum.)
- EOL: committee recommendation 15 to 20 dedicated EOL hours; Greg floated 20 to 40 hrs as
  the specialization block.
Map EOL to PQS "specialized support" (with SUD and TRD as parallel future specializations).
Missing / open: define what a "specialization hour" is and what evidence counts as
"readiness"; flag as an open T&E question.

### Finding 4: Test-out / credential-out
Already-specialized palliative clinicians should be able to test out / credential out of the
EOL hours (Larry, Greg, Christine). Analogy (Larry): buprenorphine waiver training for
primary care, special training without requiring the full addiction-medicine specialist.
Open: is it a mandatory test, or does an existing credential auto-satisfy? Flag for the
training committee.

### Finding 5: Acuity-based matching (new organizing axis)
Match practitioner skill level to medical/psychological acuity, not to diagnosis (Jamie).
Severe PTSD to advanced practitioner; newly-diagnosed low-acuity EOL to generalist;
ALS/nonverbal/hospitalized to highest skill. This sidesteps DOH's licensure objection
(acuity tiers, not diagnosis tiers). Christine: the practitioner owns a plan of care; the
patient has agency to choose pending medical clearance.

### Finding 6: Non-directive-presence competency (new baseline)
Palliative/hospice professionals are trained to actively ease suffering; psychedelic work
demands the opposite: hold still, "lean into the uncomfortableness instead of wanting to fix
it," sit in silence for hours. Michael named this as a category not yet in the curriculum.
Add an explicit baseline competency: un-learning the fix-it reflex / holding non-directive
presence.

### Finding 7: Continuity vs. fragmentation (design constraint)
Reject the "one person preps, another journeys, a third integrates, a fourth checks in"
model (called absurd by Michael; firmly opposed by Mary, the relationship is the healing).
Design for relational continuity: a death doula carrying through the journey; Space
Attendants providing volunteer continuity. Rules should caution against role fragmentation.

### Finding 8: Staffing / ratio / Space Attendant (with cost evidence)
- Best-practice ratio approximately 2:6 (one-to-three), minimum two people; group-vs-
  individual logic; being compiled into best-practice recs.
- Space Attendant model: one licensed therapist/facilitator plus one Space Attendant
  (hospice volunteer, ~50 hrs training), especially for home-based sessions (never send one
  person to a home alone; non-home may be a single facilitator, unspecified).
- Cost evidence (Mary Cosimano, Hopkins): they never required two licensed therapists; a
  skilled research assistant with presence worked; two licensed providers is a major cost
  driver. Use against any two-licensed-provider mandate.
- Workforce lever (Greg): put the second person in the room as a resident earning practicum
  hours (doula/apprenticeship model): solves cost, workforce, and experience-transfer
  simultaneously.

### Finding 9: Gender pairing to capture as data, not rule
Hopkins never required gender matching; outcomes did not differ. Given today's complexity,
capture "therapeutic alignment" (did the client feel aligned with their care team?) as an
outcomes instrument rather than a hard staffing rule (Greg).

### Finding 10: Caregiver-as-co-patient + UNM pilot
Nearly every Hopkins caregiver wanted their own experience; caregiver dynamics move outcomes
(Mary's sabotaging-spouse case). Larry: a potential UNM pilot treating both caregiver and
EOL patient, not currently covered under the state bill, pending research authorization.
Curriculum already covers caregiver dynamics (Session 6); the rules/research track needs a
caregiver-treatment pathway flag.

### Finding 11: Cross-subcommittee crosswalk rows (add as tracked rows)
- Data schema to curriculum domains: research/continuous-improvement subcommittee (Greg) is
  defining the data-extraction schema and wants it mapped to the curriculum domains. Live
  coordination item.
- Billing and coding / Medicaid: DOH draft curriculum already includes billing and coding;

- CE/CME: Christine's courses carry CEs for nurses and social workers; CMEs pending.

- Existential/spiritual harm: added to the ethics section (Session 8) per Jamie Beachy;
  closed action item.

### Finding 12: Interim (year-one) vs. steady-state posture
Until NM has its own program, residents arrive via reciprocity from Colorado/Oregon, where
EOL training may be only a couple of hours (Larry). Hanifa: for year one, focus on
reciprocity for EOL and revisit rules once the NM program is operational; codifying now is
premature. Barak Wolf (EMS-certification-development experience): maximum flexibility early,
do not over-burden current practitioners; self-selection pulls motivated people into adjunct
trainings. Distinguish a year-one interim posture (reciprocity plus adjunct endorsement)
from a steady-state posture (possibly baked-into-core once NM training exists, Larry's
position, since EOL is 1 of 4 qualifying conditions).

### Highest-value fix (internal)
Resolve the DACPC spiritual-care no-slot gap: chaplains/doulas do continuity work with no
post-training role. The transcript proves it and Larry/Michael/Mary all argue for exactly
that continuity role. Name it explicitly to T&E and give it a home in the model (likely as a
Space Attendant / continuity-role endorsement, not a new license).
